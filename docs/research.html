Research

My research studies how institutions, infrastructure, and markets shaped long-run development, with a primary focus on nineteenth-century France. I am particularly interested in spatially uneven modernisation: why the same shocks that integrated rural areas into national markets, schools, language, and politics generated convergence in some places but concentration, migration, and deindustrialisation in others. Empirically, I construct original historical datasets from archival, textual, administrative, and geospatial sources, combining quantitative methods with GIS, OCR, computer vision, and reproducible workflows.

Doctoral Project

Diffusion and Divergence: Railways, Schools, and the Modernisation of Rural France

Doctoral Project Economic History Spatial & Regional Economics

Planned supervision: Roberto Galbiati; with potential co-supervision from Pierre-Philippe Combes and Kevin O’Rourke

This project asks how railway-induced market integration and mass primary education reshaped the geography of development in nineteenth-century rural France. The dissertation treats railways and schooling as modernisation shocks that integrated rural communes into national markets, institutions, and language, while also reallocating people, firms, capital, and activity across space. The central question is why these shocks generated integration and convergence in some places but concentration, out-migration, and deindustrialisation in others.

  • Railways, Schools, and the Making of France Studies whether railway expansion produced linguistic integration and political mobilisation, and how railway access interacted with primary schooling.
  • Education and the Restructuring of Rural Industry Extends my master’s thesis by asking whether the Guizot Law’s schooling shock accumulated human capital locally or reallocated it toward industrialising centres.
  • Railways, Market Access, and Competition Connects the market-access and trade-shock literatures to study when railway integration generated expansion, restructuring, or decline in agriculture and industry.

Master’s Thesis

“Enrichissez-vous!”: Education, Suffrage, and Local Development in July Monarchy France

Master’s Thesis · Sciences Po · 2025 Sciences Po Prize for Best Master’s Thesis (2024-2025)

Jury: Roberto Galbiati, Emeric Henry (supervisors), Kevin O’Rourke

Abstract This paper studies how primary schooling and municipal suffrage shaped local development in July Monarchy France. I combine the 1831 Municipal Law and the 1833 Guizot Law with an arrondissement-level dataset covering 1830-1865. Pre-reform male primary education is positively associated with industrial wages in the 1840s, but I find no robust independent or interactive effect of municipal suffrage and no statistically significant medium-run wage effect of policy-induced schooling expansion. The paper also shows that threshold-based designs in this setting face historically informative identification challenges because commune-size distributions are spatially structured and correlated with prior development.
